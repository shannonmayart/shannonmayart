Sainsburys

PDF Version
Summary
​Sainsburys is a large shopping superstore serving around 12.5 million customers weekly. With such a broad customer base, keeping design inclusive while still innovating poses a unique challenge. The premise for this case study was to envision a software update for Sainsburys' self checkouts for new hardware that does not weigh items in the bagging area - A decision that could possibly lead to more shoplifting, but less abandoned baskets. There were also some key data-driven hypotheses listed in the problem statement below.
​What's the Problem? What can this redesign solve?
•  Shoppers get frustrated with the bagging area not weighing items correctly, slowing down their checkout experience.
​•  Nectar discount card signups have been declining.
•  Customers find looking through the list of scanned basket items on screen difficult.
•  Complex transition animations frustrate customers as the hardware the self checkouts are running on is slow and sometimes freezes.
Interactive Prototype
•  Use spacebar to simulate an action that does not use the screen (for example, scanning an item or using a debit card)
•  Click on the nectar card symbol in the top right of the basket or payment screen to simulate scanning a Nectar card.
Design Review
WHAT WORKS
  • Clean UI with clear delineated sections, making navigation easy for the user.
  • Large buttons for ease of pressing make the software very accessible.
  • Clear use of Sainsburys branding that we want to take forward and perhaps even enhance with Sainsbury’s secondary brand colour.
WHAT COULD BE IMPROVED
  • Visual clarity of the list of scanned items. This could be improved with actual product images.
  • Nectar card integration is minimal, and could be higher to encourage future signups.
  • Slow transitions between checkout pages could be mitigated with simpler animations, or eliminating unnecessary animations.
  • The path to remove items from the basket is not clear. We do not want to directly encourage this behavior, but we do not want to make it unnecessarily difficult.
User Journey
I mapped out the new user journey, which deviated from the original as weighing scales were no longer involved. Upon realising there is an issue with where to leave products if a transaction is cancelled, I theorised the use of a 'put-back trolley', a place where shoppers can place their unwanted items for an assistant to put back on the shelves later. This would improve the user experience outside of the use of the software itself.

Unhappy paths and error states
​​​​Users are not always going to have a perfect experience - this user journey also covers the unhappy paths of a shopper, such as having to cancel an item or a payment is declined.
Information Architecture
Nectar Discounts
Discounted prices are now displayed beneath the standard item price - As well as the prompt in the upper right corner, this encourages the user to scan their nectar card to obtain these discounts. There is also an additional nectar card nudge on the payment screen to give a shopper the maximum number of chances to scan their card.
Item Image Integration
Using data from the existing online Sainsburys product database, preview images can be used during checkout to visually confirm the item that the shopper has just scanned.
Accessibility
WCAG Validation
The checkout software design is compliant with the 2.3 Web Content Accessibility Guidelines - This includes:
  • Legible text sizes and large buttons
  • AA Standard colour contrast ratios
  • Short load / response times
​
VISUAL ACCESSIBILITY
The design was tested using visual impairment simulation tools - the new design is suitable for colour blind users, users with light sensitivity, and users with minor vision acuity.

PERFORMANCE
Animations are now only used on the initial screen, and the final screen of the system to minimise lag and help prevent crashes (This gives a better user impression at both the start and end of their experience, and improves usability during the actual scanning and payment stage)
Wireframing
​The wireframes were achieved with a combination of figma tools and Sainsburys’ design system Luna, to make sure that the final design followed Sainsburys existing brand style.
